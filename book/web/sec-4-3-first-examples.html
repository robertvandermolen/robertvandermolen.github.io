    Section 3.3 Our First Proof

    Now that we have our first weapon in hand (the direct proof) lets start to slay our first dragon.
    Prove: For all integers \(x\text{,}\) whenever \(x\) is odd then \(x+1\) is even.
    Before we begin any proof we sing our song (play-along):
    "Whats the \({\color{red}{P}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 1.
    \(x\) is odd
    "Whats the \({\color{blue}{Q}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 2.
    \(x+1\) is even
    "Whatre the definitions?"
    Answer 3.
    Odd (Definition3.1.2) and even (Definition3.1.1)!
    "Now, what to do?"
    Answer 4.
    A direct proof! (it is the only method we know so far)

    Section 3.3.1 \({\color{red}{\text{The Beginning}}}\)

    One purpose of our fun song is for us to to split what we want to prove into the \({\color{red}{\text{Beginning}}}\) and the \({\color{blue}{\text{End}}}\text{.}\)
    Once we have it split like that we see something left over... this is the \({\color{orange}{\text{beginning quantifier}}}\text{.}\) This quantifier is quite often forgotten/assumed when it is not written but assumed there we call it the hidden quantifier. Either way the statement we are trying to prove is now broken up as follows:
    \begin{equation*} {\color{orange}{\forall x\in\Z}}\text{ if }{\color{red}{x\text{ is odd}}}\text{ then }{\color{blue}{x+1\text{ is even}}} \end{equation*}
    To prove our statment with this \({\color{orange}{\text{beginning quantifier}}}\text{,}\) according to Universal Generalization from Figure2.4.1 we need to show it works with an arbitrary integer.
    To indicate this in our proof we may begin with either of the senctences:
    "Let \(x\) be an integer."
    or
    "Choose an arbitrary integer \(x\)"
    Now, we can take care of the \({\color{red}{\text{Beginning}}}\) from the direct proof:
    Assume the "\({\color{red}{P}}\)"
    "\({\color{red}{\text{Assume that }x\text{ is odd}}}\text{.}\)"

    Section 3.3.2 \({\color{green}{\text{The Muddle}}}\)

    To proceed with our proof we dont have much expcept the definitions. (It can be helpful, especially in these early stages of proof writing, to write the relevant definitions to the side in your scratch work - this is why its part of the song!)
    From the definition odd (Definition3.1.2) we can make the conclusion that:
    \(x\) must look like:
    \begin{equation*} x=2k+1 \end{equation*}
    for some integer \(k\text{.}\)
    Invoking this definition in this manner is an application of Existential Instantiation from Figure2.4.1
    To continue muddling along we can use some college algebra, as is allowed by Section3.1.1 and calculate:
    \begin{align*} x+1\amp=(2k+1)+1\\ \amp=k+2\\ \amp=2(k+1) \end{align*}
    The \(x+1\) on the left of this equation is the end conclusion were aiming for! ("Whats the \({\color{blue}{Q}}\)" from the song)
    The stuff on the right is what we manipulate to make it "look like," or "fit," the definition of even. We will attempt to make this more clear in the next subsection.

    Remark 3.3.1.

    I choose these as the first types of proofs to show/have students do first, because the \({\color{green}{\text{muddle}}}\) is bascially the same for all of them: "invoke a defintion then calculate". It gives the students something to hold on to as they are still trying to digest the previous chapters...

    Section 3.3.3 \({\color{blue}{\text{The End}}}\)

    From the \({\color{green}{\text{muddle}}}\text{,}\) we have:
    \begin{equation*} x+1=2{\color{purple}{(k+1)}} \end{equation*}
    To be able to make our conclusion
    \begin{equation*} {\color{blue}{Q}}:\text{ } x+1 \text{ is even} \end{equation*}
    we must use defintion of even (Definition3.1.1), which uses an existential quantifier so we must (Figure2.4.1) be able to produce/point at an integer, so that \(x+1\) is this integer times 2.
    We have exactly that, because (Section3.1.1) \({\color{purple}{k+1}}\) is an integer!
    To write this in our proof we can say:
    "Since \({\color{purple}{k+1}}\) is an integer, by definition of even, \(x+1\) is even."
    Finally we should end every proof with a wrap-up sentence. This sentence is to summarize the proof, most importantly laying out your proving method, in this example a direct proof.
    For this proof our wrap-up could look like:
    "Since we assumed that an arbitrary integer \(x\) was odd and showed that \(x+1\) is even, we can conclude that for all integers \(x\text{,}\) whenever \(x\) is odd then \(x+1\) is even, by direct proof."
    At last we indicate that our proofs are over with a:
    \begin{equation*} \text{ QED } \end{equation*}
    To wrap-up everything in a pretty bow, lets see the whole proof in one spot.
    When a proof is finished, customarily, we either write "QED" or \(\square\) at the bottom. This comes from the Latin "quod erat demonstrandum," which means "that which was to be demonstrated."

    Example 3.3.1.

    Prove: For all integers \(x\text{,}\) whenever \(x\) is odd then \(x+1\) is even.
    Let \(x\) be an integer. Assume \(x\) is odd. Thus, by the definition of odd, we can find an integer \(k\) so that \(x=2k+1\text{.}\)
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} x+1\amp=(2k+1)+1\\ \amp=k+2\\ \amp=2(k+1) \end{align*}
    Since \(k+1\) is an integer, by definition of even, \(x+1\) is even.
    Since we assumed that and arbitrary integer \(x\) was odd and showed that \(x+1\) is even, we can conclude that for all integers \(x\text{,}\) whenever \(x\) is odd then \(x+1\) is even, by direct proof. \(\square\)
