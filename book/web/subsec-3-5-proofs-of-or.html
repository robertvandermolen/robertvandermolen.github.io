    Section 3.5 Proofs with Conjunctions and Disjunctions

    So far we have been proving the prototypical
    \begin{equation*} P\implies Q \end{equation*}
    as it is perhaps one of the most common propositional forms used in mathematics, and otherwise. Yet, in all of our examples thus far both \(P\) and \(Q\) have been atomic propositions. In this section we will be substituting the antecedent and consequent with compound propositions.

    Assuming an Or.

    For propositions \(P\text{,}\) \(Q\text{,}\) and \(R\text{,}\) to prove a statement in the form of
    \begin{equation*} (P\vee R)\implies Q \end{equation*}
    First we:
    Prove: \(P\implies Q\)
    then we:
    Prove: \(R\implies Q\)
    To verify that this argument is valid, we will use a truth table to show:
    \begin{equation*} [(P\implies Q)\wedge(R\implies Q)]\implies [(P\vee R)\implies Q] \end{equation*}
    is a tautology. To do so we will show a stronger result using the tricks of Subsection 1.4.3.
    \begin{align*} (P\vee R)\implies Q\amp \equiv [\sim (P\vee R)]\vee Q \text{ (Rob's Law)}\\ \amp \equiv [(\sim P)\wedge (\sim R)]\vee Q \text{ (De Morgan's)}\\ \amp \equiv [(\sim P)\vee Q]\wedge[ (\sim R)\vee Q ]\text{ (distribution)}\\ \amp \equiv [P\implies Q]\wedge[R\implies Q] \text{ (Rob's Law)} \end{align*}
    Now lets see an example of this in play.

    Example 3.5.1.

    Prove: for any two integers \(x\) and \(y\) if \(x\) is even or \(y\) is even then \(x\cdot y\) is even.

    Proof.

    Let \(x\) and \(y\) be integers.
    First assume that \(x\) is even. By definition of even (Definition 3.1.1) we can find an integer \(k\) so that \(x=2k\text{.}\) Calculate:
    \begin{align*} x\cdot y\amp=(2k)\cdot y\\ \amp=2(ky) \end{align*}
    since \(ky\) is an integer by the definition of even, \(x\cdot y\) is even.
    Next, we instead assume \(y\) is even. By definition of even we can find an integer \(t\) so that \(y=2t\text{.}\) Calculate:
    \begin{align*} x\cdot y\amp=x\cdot(2t)\\ \amp=2(xt) \end{align*}
    since \(xt\) is an integer by the definition of even, \(x\cdot y\) is even.
    Now since we have shown that for \(\forall x,y\in\Z\) that both \(x\) is even implies \(x\cdot y\) is even and \(y\) is even implies \(x\cdot y\) is even, we have shown that \(\forall x,y\in\Z\text{.}\)
    Another interesting scenario is when we are trying to conclude a disjunction.

    Concluding an Or.

    For propositions \(P\text{,}\) \(Q\text{,}\) and \(R\text{,}\) to prove a statement in the form of
    \begin{equation*} P\implies (Q\vee R) \end{equation*}
    We instead
    Prove: \([P\wedge(\sim R)]\implies Q\)
    That this argument is valid can be seen by showing that
    \begin{equation*} [P\implies(Q\vee R)]\equiv[[P\wedge(\sim R)]\implies Q] \end{equation*}
    To do that we will again use the tricks of Subsection 1.4.3.
    \begin{align*} P\implies (Q\vee R)\amp \equiv (\sim P)\vee( Q\vee R) \text{ (Rob's Law)}\\ \amp \equiv (\sim P)\vee (R\vee Q) \text{ (commutativity)}\\ \amp \equiv [(\sim P)\vee R]\vee Q \text{ (associativity)}\\ \amp \equiv \sim[P\wedge (\sim R)]\vee Q \text{ (De Morgan's)}\\ \amp \equiv (P\wedge (\sim R))\implies Q \text{ (Rob's Law)} \end{align*}
    Now lets see an example.

    Example 3.5.2.

    Prove: For all integers \(x\) and \(y\) if \(x\) is even then \(y\) is odd or \(x+y\) is even

    Proof.

    Let \(x\) and \(y\) be arbitrary integers. Assume that \(x\) is even and that \(y\) is not odd. By the definition of even (Definition 3.1.1) we can find an integer \(k\) so that \(x=2k\text{.}\) By the division algorithm (Subsection 3.1.1) we can find integers \(q\) and \(r\) such that \(0\leq r\lt 2\) such that \(y=2q+r\text{.}\) Yet since we assumed \(y\) is not odd \(r\neq 1\text{,}\) and since \(r\) is an integer and \(0\leq r\lt 2\) and the only integers which satisfy this inequality are 0 and 1, which only leaves \(r=0\) thus \(y=2q\text{.}\) Now calculate:
    \begin{align*} x+y\amp=2k+2q\\ \amp=2(k+q) \end{align*}
    Since \(k+q\) is an integer by the definition of even \(x+y\) is even.
    Since we have assumed for two arbitrary integers \(x\) and \(y\) that \(x\) is even and \(y\) is not odd, and we have concluded that \(x+y\) is even, we can conclude that for all integers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(x\) is even then \(y\) is odd or \(x+y\) is even.
    The last propositional form we consider in this section is the following.

    Concluding an And.

    For propositions \(P\text{,}\) \(Q\text{,}\) and \(R\text{,}\) one way to prove a statment in the form of
    \begin{equation*} P\implies (Q\wedge R) \end{equation*}
    First we:
    Prove: \(P\implies Q\)
    then we:
    Prove: \(P\implies R\)
    To see that this is a valid way of proving, we once again show an even stronger result using the methods of Subsection 1.4.3.
    \begin{align*} P\implies (Q\wedge R)\amp \equiv (\sim P)\vee( Q\wedge R) \text{ (Rob's Law)}\\ \amp \equiv [(\sim P)\vee Q]\wedge [(\sim P)\vee R] \text{ (distribution)}\\ \amp \equiv [P\implies Q]\wedge [P\implies R] \text{ (Rob's Law)} \end{align*}

    Example 3.5.3.

    Prove: For all integers \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) if \(3|(a-2)\) and \(3|(b-1)\) then \(3|(a+b)\) and \(3|(a-2b)\)

    Proof.

    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be arbitrary integers.
    Assume that \(3|(a-2)\text{,}\) also assume that \(3|(b-1)\text{.}\) By definition of divides (Definition 3.1.3) we can produce an integer \(k\) such that \(a-2=3k\text{,}\) and hence by adding 2 to both sides of this equation we get \(a=3k+2\text{.}\) As well by the definition of divides we can come forth with another integer \(t\) with the property that \(b-1=3t\text{,}\) this time adding 1 to both sides of the equation we see that \(b=3t+1\text{.}\)
    Now, we will prove: if \(3|(a-2)\) and \(3|(b-1)\) then \(3|(a+b)\text{,}\) by calculating:
    \begin{align*} a+b\amp=(3k+2)+(3t+1)\\ \amp=3k+3t+3\\ \amp=3(k+t+1) \end{align*}
    and since \(k+t+1\) is an integer by the definition of divides \(3|(a+b)\text{.}\)
    Next, we will prove: if \(3|(a-2)\) and \(3|(b-1)\) then \(3|(a-2b)\text{,}\) instead by calculating:
    \begin{align*} a+b\amp=(3k+2)-2(3t+1)\\ \amp=3k+2-6t-2\\ \amp=3k-6t\\ \amp=3(k-2t) \end{align*}
    and since \(k-2t\) is an integer by the definition of divides \(3|(a-2b)\text{.}\)
    Now that we have assumed that \(a\) and \(b\) are arbitrary integers and assumed that \(3|(a-2)\) and \(3|(b-1)\) then we showed that both \(3|(a+b)\) and that \(3|(a-2b)\) we can conclude that for all integers \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) if \(3|(a-2)\) and \(3|(b-1)\) then \(3|(a+b)\) and \(3|(a-2b)\text{.}\)
