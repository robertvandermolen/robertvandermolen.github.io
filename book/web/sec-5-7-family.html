    Section 5.7 Families

    We now consider basic constructions like union, intersection, and the cross product between many sets at once. We consider these over a family of sets, which is just a fancy way of saying a set whose members are sets. We will most often use the script font to indicate a family, for example, \(\mathscr{A}\text{.}\)

    Definition 5.7.1. Union over a Family.

    Let \(\mathscr{A}\) be a family of sets. We define the union over \(\mathscr{A}\) as
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{A\in\mathscr{A}} A=\{x\text{ }|\text{ }x\in A\text{ for some }A\in\mathscr{A}\} \end{equation*}
    As we are still learning set-builder notation, to help us be able to unpack the condition in the previous definition, note the following condition of membership:
    \begin{equation*} x\in\bigcup_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\text{ }\text{ if and only if }\text{ }[\exists A\in\mathscr{A}\text{ }x\in A] \end{equation*}

    Example 5.7.2.

    We now illustrate the union over a family with a Venn diagram below for the family of sets \(\mathscr{R}=\{A,B,C\}\)

    Example 5.7.3.

    Consider the family of sets \(\mathscr{B}=\{R,S,T\}\) where each set is defined as follows
    \begin{equation*} R=\{1,3,G,\square,\triangle,\Omega\} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} S=\{\alpha, \Gamma, H, 7, 900\} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} T=\{1, \Gamma, \triangle, 200, 16\} \end{equation*}
    Thus the union over \(\mathscr{B}\) is
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{B\in\mathscr{B}}B=\left(R\cup S\right)\cup T=\{1,3,G,\square,\triangle,\Omega, \alpha, \Gamma, H, 7, 900, 200, 16\} \end{equation*}
    Our next topic examines intersections within this new concept of families of sets.

    Definition 5.7.4. Intersection over a Family.

    Let \(\mathscr{A}\) be a family of sets. We define the intersection over \(\mathscr{A}\) as
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{A\in\mathscr{A}}=\{x\text{ }|\text{ }x\in A\text{ for every }A\in\mathscr{A}\} \end{equation*}
    To help again, we unpack the condition in the previous definition, note the following condition of membership:
    \begin{equation*} x\in\bigcap_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\text{ }\text{ if and only if }\text{ }[\forall A\in\mathscr{A}\text{ }x\in A] \end{equation*}

    Example 5.7.5.

    We now illustrate the intersection over a family with Venn diagram below, again, for the family of sets \(\mathscr{R}=\{A,B,C\}\)

    Example 5.7.6.

    Consider the family of sets \(\mathscr{B}=\{R,S,T\}\) where each set is defined as follows
    \begin{equation*} R=\{1,3,G,\square,\triangle,\Omega\} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} S=\{3, \alpha, \Gamma, H, \Omega, 900, \triangle\} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} T=\{1,3, \Gamma,\Omega, \triangle, 200, 16\} \end{equation*}
    Thus the union over \(\mathscr{B}\) is
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{B\in\mathscr{B}}B=\left(R\cap S\right)\cap T=\{\Omega, 3, \triangle\} \end{equation*}
    Now, lets explore a general proof involving these constructions.

    Example 5.7.7.

    Prove: For a non-empty family of sets \(\mathscr{A}\)
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\subseteq\bigcup_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    We begin as we do for any subset proof, and that is, we choose an arbitrary element \(x\in\bigcap_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\)
    By definition of the intersection over a family, for any member of the family, \(x\) must belong to it. So choose an arbitrary \(C\in\mathscr{A}\text{,}\) thus \(x\in C\text{,}\) which by definition of union, since \(x\in C\) we have that \(x\in\bigcup_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\text{.}\)
    Therefore since we chose an arbitrary element \(x\in\bigcap_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\) and have shown \(x\in\bigcup_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\text{,}\) we can conclude \(\bigcap_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\subseteq\bigcup_{A\in\mathscr{A}}A\) as desired. \(\square\)
    If this is still a bit too abstract for our dear reader, we will now introduce a concept which allows us to look at these constructions a bit more like the summation from our calculus classes.

    Definition 5.7.8. Indexed Family of Sets.

    Let \(\Delta\) be a nonempty set such that for every \(i\in\Delta\) we correspond a set \(A_i\text{,}\) an indexed family of sets over \(\Delta\text{,}\) is the family of sets,
    \begin{equation*} \mathscr{A}=\{A_i\text{ }|\text{ }i\in\Delta\} \end{equation*}
    We call the set \(\Delta\) the indexing set.
    The most common case of an index set is when \(\Delta\subseteq\N\) in the form of
    \begin{equation*} \Delta=\{0,1,2,3,...,n\} \end{equation*}
    for some \(n\in\N\text{,}\) in this case we will often write the intersections as
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{i\in\Delta}A_i=\bigcap_{i=0}^n A_i \end{equation*}
    and the union as
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i=\bigcup_{i=0}^n A_i \end{equation*}
    With this new construction, lets visit another example.

    Example 5.7.9.

    Consider the indexing set \(\Delta=\{0,1,2\}\text{,}\) and the family of sets \(\mathscr{C}=\{C_0,C_1,C_2\}\) where each set is defined as follows:
    \begin{equation*} C_0=\left\{7,15,H,\triangle,\pi,\frac{1}{3}\right\} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} C_1=\left\{15, \triangle, \square, H, \frac{1}{3}, 0, \pi\right\} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} C_2=\left\{7,15, \Gamma,\frac{1}{3}, \pi, 0, 16\right\} \end{equation*}
    Thus, the union over \(\mathscr{C}\) is
    \begin{align*} \bigcap_{i\in\Delta}=C_i\bigcap_{i=0}^{2}C_i\amp =\left(C_0\cap C_1\right)\cap C_2\\ \amp =\left\{\frac{1}{3}, 15, \pi\right\} \end{align*}
    Of course this basic use case of an index set is far from the only one. In the next example, we leave the case of a finite indexing set by setting \(\Delta=\N\text{.}\) This following example also illustrates perhaps the most common use case in your future math courses, with each set in our family being a subset of the real numbers.

    Example 5.7.10.

    Consider the indexing set \(\N\) and the family of sets \(\mathscr{A}=\{A_i\text{ }|\text{ }i\in\N\}\) defined as the following intervals,
    \begin{equation*} A_i=\left[\left.\frac{1}{i+2},\frac{1}{i+1}\right.\right)=\left\{x\in\R\text{ }\left|\text{ }\frac{1}{i+1}\leq x\lt \frac{1}{i}\right.\right\} \end{equation*}
    thus we have
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{i\in\N}A_i=\bigcup_{i=0}^\infty A_i=(0,1)=\left\{x\in\R\text{ }|\text{ }0\lt x\lt 1\right\} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{i\in\N}A_i=\bigcap_{i=0}^\infty A_i=\emptyset \end{equation*}
    In your next example, we leave the natural numbers for our index set, and instead explore the example where our indexing set is \(\Q\text{.}\)

    Example 5.7.11.

    Consider the the indexing set \(\Q\text{.}\) Now define the indexed family of sets \(\mathscr{B}=\{B_n\text{ }|\text{ }n\in\Gamma\}\text{,}\) defined as the following closed intervals,
    \begin{equation*} B_\alpha=\big[\alpha,\alpha+1\big]=\{x\in\R\text{ }|\text{ }\alpha\leq x\leq \alpha+1\} \end{equation*}
    thus we have
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{\alpha\in\Q}B_\alpha=[0,\infty)=\{x\in\R\text{ }|\text{ }0\leq x \lt\infty\} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{\alpha\in\Q}B_\alpha=\{0\} \end{equation*}
    Now that we have built our intuition on unions and intersections over families, lets prove a result about them.

    Example 5.7.12.

    Let \(\Delta\) be an indexing set, and \(\mathscr{A}=\{A_i\text{ }|\text{ }i\in\Delta\}\) be an indexed family of sets.
    Prove: If \(B\) is a set then
    \begin{equation*} B\cap \left(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i\right)=\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}(B\cap A_i) \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    This is a proof of the equality of two sets so we will need to break up the proof into two parts, namely we will need to show two subsets.
    Proving: \(B\cap \left(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i\right)\subseteq\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}(B\cap A_i)\)
    To prove this, we will start like most subset proofs, namely by choosing an arbitrary element \(s\in B\cap \left(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i\right)\text{.}\) By the definition of intersection, both \(s\in B\) and \(s\in\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i\text{.}\) By the definition of union over a family, if we choose an arbitrary \(j\in\Delta\) we have \(s\in A_j\text{.}\) Thus, since \(s\in B\) and \(s\in A_j\text{,}\) by the definition of intersection we have that \(s\in B\cap A_j\text{.}\) Because we chose \(j\in\Delta\) arbitrarily, it is true that for any \(i\in\Delta\) \(s\in B\cap A_i\text{.}\) Hence, the definition of union over a family is satisfied, that is:
    \begin{equation*} s\in\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}(B\cap A_i) \end{equation*}
    as desired.
    Proving: \(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}(B\cap A_i)\subseteq B\cap \left(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i\right)\)
    To prove this, we will start like most subset proofs, namely by choosing an arbitrary element \(y\in \bigcup_{i\in\Delta}(B\cap A_i)\text{.}\) By definition of union over a family when we choose an arbitrary element \(k\in\Delta\) we must have that \(y\in (B\cap A_k)\text{.}\) By definition of intersection, this means that both \(y\in B\) and \(y\in A_k\text{.}\) Since we chose \(k\in\Delta\) arbitrarily, we have shown that for any \(i\in\Delta\) that \(y\in A_i\text{.}\) That is, we have shown the definition of membership to a union over a family, namely that
    \begin{equation*} y\in\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i \end{equation*}
    Yet, we have also shown that \(y\in B\text{,}\) thus by definition of intersection we have that
    \begin{equation*} y\in B\cap\left(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i)\right) \end{equation*}
    as desired.
    Since we have shown both the subsets
    \begin{equation*} B\cap \left(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i\right)\subseteq\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}(B\cap A_i) \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{i\in\Delta}(B\cap A_i)\subseteq B\cap \left(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i\right) \end{equation*}
    we can conclude the desired equality of:
    \begin{equation*} B\cap \left(\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}A_i\right)=\bigcup_{i\in\Delta}(B\cap A_i) \end{equation*}
    \(\square\)
    We finish up this section with a concept which will come into play when we discuss partitions later in the course.

    Definition 5.7.13. Pairwise Disjoint.

    The indexed family \(\mathscr{A} = \{A_\alpha | \alpha \in \Delta\}\) of sets is pairwise disjoint if and only if for all \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) in \(\Delta\text{,}\) either
    \begin{equation*} A_\alpha = A_\beta \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} A_\alpha \cap A_\beta = \emptyset \end{equation*}
    In other words, the sets are completely the same, or completely different.

    Example 5.7.14.

    First consider the indexing set \(\Delta=\{1,2,3\}\) and the indexed family of sets \(\mathscr{A}=\{A_1,A_2,A_3\}\)
    Notice that
    \begin{equation*} A_1\cap A_2=\emptyset \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} A_1\cap A_3=\emptyset \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} A_2\cap A_3=\emptyset \end{equation*}
    and thus this family is pairwise disjoint.
    Next, consider the same index set of \(\Delta=\{1,2,3\}\) yet this time the indexed family \(\mathscr{B}=\{B_1,B_2,B_3\}\)
    This time notice that
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{i\in\Delta}B_i=\emptyset \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} B_2\cap B_3=\emptyset \end{equation*}
    yet
    \begin{equation*} B_1\cap B_3\neq\emptyset \end{equation*}
    yet
    \begin{equation*} B_1\neq B_3 \end{equation*}
    thus \(\mathscr{B}\) is not pairwise disjoint
    We could not end the section without a proof, so here is your obligatory proof.

    Example 5.7.15.

    Let \(\Delta\) be an indexing set, and let both \(\mathscr{A}=\{A_i\text{ }|\text{ }i\in\Delta\}\) and \(\mathscr{B}=\{B_i\text{ }|\text{ }i\in\Delta\}\) be indexed family of sets.
    Prove: If \(\mathscr{A}\) is pairwise disjoint and \(\mathscr{B}\subseteq\mathscr{A}\) then \(\mathscr{B}\) is pairwise disjoint.

    Proof.

    We begin this proof by assuming our antecedent.
    Assume that \(\mathscr{A}\) is pairwise disjoint and \(\mathscr{B}\subseteq\mathscr{A}\text{.}\) It is our objective to prove that \(\mathscr{B}\) is pairwise disjoint, but looking above and seeing the definition of pairwise disjoint, we see that we need to prove a universal quantifier, thus we choose arbitrary \(j\in\Delta\) and \(k\in\Delta\text{.}\) Now, since we have assumed that \(\mathscr{B}\subseteq\mathscr{A}\) we have that both \(B_j\in\mathscr{A}\) and \(B_k\in\mathscr{A}\text{,}\) thus by definition of membership to \(\mathscr{A}\) we can find \(s\in\Delta\) and \(t\in\Delta\) so that \(B_j=A_s\) and \(B_k=A_t\text{.}\)
    Since we have assumed that \(\mathscr{A}\) is pairwise disjoint then either
    \begin{equation*} B_j\cap B_k=A_s\cap A_t=\emptyset \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} B_j=A_s=A_t=B_k \end{equation*}
    hence we have satisfied the definition of pairwise disjoint of \(\mathscr{B}\text{.}\) \(\square\)

    Note 5.7.16.

    For the proofs in this section we see a common \({\color{green}{\text{muddle}}}\) that I like to call chasing the definitions. I know it can be jarring at first not having your warm and safe calculative muddle, but I promise you it will be ok! And I promise you in the next chapter there will always be a very solid calculative step!
