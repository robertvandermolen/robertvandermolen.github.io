    Section 6.4 The Fibonacci Sequence

    This next topic is one that stuck with me from my undergraduate experience. I was lucky enough to be taught by the Italian mathematician Dr. Annalisa Calini. She told us the story of her fellow Italian mathematician Fibonacci with such enthusiasm that I could never hope to match. Yet, here is my poor attempt.

    The Story of Fibonacci.

    Our story begins on Day 1 with Fibonacci sitting in a park on a beautiful day. He notices 1 pair young bunnies, male and female, hopping by.
    The next day, on Day 2 Fibonacci returned to the same park and noticed the same 1 pair, yet this time they have grown to rabbits, now biologically capable of procreating.
    He again returned the following day, on Day 3, and on this day he noticed his pair of rabbits, but also another pair of bunnies, again paired as in one male and one female, giving us a total of 2 pair of rabbits.
    Now on Day 4 Fibonacci noticed his original pair had given birth again, as well as the first litter was now grown to the age of reproduction, giving us 3 pairs of rabbits total. He deduced that it took one day for the rabbits to mature, and then another day for the rabbits to give birth to a new pair of bunnies.
    On Day 5 returning once again to this same park he noticed that his original pair was there, and their first litter but not the original pair and the first litter have both produced pairs of bunnies, leaving a total of 5 pairs of rabbits at the park.
    Later on Day 5, Fibonacci returns to his home and comes up with perhaps the greatest mathematical assumption in all of history,
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{\text{ Assume that Rabbits never die! }} \end{equation*}
    Then asked himself how many rabbits would be there on day 6, or 7, or 8, or even 1000...
    To create a formal defintion of the fibonacci we use the concept of inductive definitons. An inductive defintion is one which takes the form of PMI, specifically where we first define a base case and then we define the inductive step

    Definition 6.4.1. The Fibonacci Numbers.

    (i) (Bases Cases)
    \begin{equation*} f_1=1 \text{ and } f_2=1 \end{equation*}
    (ii) (Inductive Case)
    To calculate any Fibonacci number, \(f_n\text{,}\) we add the two numbers that preceded it:
    \begin{equation*} f_n = f_{n-1} + f_{n-2} \end{equation*}
    For fun lets count some more rabbits.

    Example 6.4.2.

    By definition
    \begin{align*} f_1 \amp =1 \\ f_2 \amp=1 \end{align*}
    then
    \begin{align*} f_3 \amp=f_2+f_1=1+1=2 \\ f_4 \amp=f_3+f_2=2+1=3 \\ f_5 \amp=f_4+f_3=3+2=5 \\ f_6 \amp=f_5+f_4=5+3=8 \\ f_7 \amp=f_6+f_5=8+5=13 \\ f_8 \amp=f_7+f_6=13+8=21 \end{align*}
    so many bunnies...
    The awake student, usually sitting somemwhere right off the front row, will notice that when we defined the Fibonacci numbers we didnt just have a singular base case, and we diddnt define the \(n+1\) case, which is slightly different from the procedure of induction. To this student I applaud you, that is correct we instead used a slightly different version of indcution, known commonly as complete induction.

    Definition 6.4.3. Principle of Complete Induction.

    Let \(k\in\N\) and suppose \(S\) is a subset of \(\mathbb{N}\) with the following property:
    \(\forall n\in \mathbb{N}\) with \(k\lt n\)
    if \(\{k,k+1,k+2,k+3,...,n-1\} \subset S\) then \(n \in S\)
    Then \(S=\{n\in\N\text{ }|\text{ }n\geq k\}\)
    Proving using complete induction amounts to a procedure almost identical to that of our traditional induction

    Proof of \(\forall m\in\N\) \(m\geq k\) \(P(m)\) with Complete Induction.

    Proof:
    1. (Base Steps) Show that \(P(k)\) and \(P(k+1)\) are true
    2. (Induction Assumption) Assume we can find an \(n\in\N\) such that \(P(t)\) is true for any \(t\in\N\) such that \(k\leq t\lt n\)
    3. \(\mathbf{\left(\text{Prove: }P(n)\right)}\) ... thus \(P(n)\) is true
    Therefore \(\forall m\in\N\) such that \(m\geq k\) then \(P(m)\) is true.
    The astute student will note we are really doing nothing different here... as in PMI we said for any \(n\) so why not up to any \(n-1\text{.}\) But, this really allows us to do exactly what we have been doing its just that in step (iii) it becomes quite cumbersome to balance \(n+1\) everywhere, so this way just lets us use \(n\) as well sometimes we can just get some number less than \(n\) and it is there we would like to invoke the induction assumption not hitting exactly one number. This range version is much more versitile and it is the method that I personally just always default to.

    Example 6.4.4.

    Prove: For any \(m\in\N\) such that \(m\geq 1\) we have \(f_{3m}\) is even (every third fibonacci number is even)

    Proof.

    (i) (Base Cases):
    As we are to show this result for any \(m\geq 1\text{,}\) our first base case will be 1!
    \begin{equation*} f_{3\cdot 1}=f_2+f_1=1+1=2 \end{equation*}
    and since 1 is an integer and thus \(2=2\cdot 1\) is even.
    As we are using PCI we need to show our next base case of 2.
    \begin{equation*} f_{3\cdot (2)}=f_6=f_5+f_4=8 \end{equation*}
    (see Example6.4.2 for the complete calculation of \(f_6\)) and since 4 is an integer and thus \(8=2\cdot 4\) is even.
    Hence we have established the base cases:
    \begin{equation*} P(1): f_{3\cdot(1)}\text{ is even} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} P(2):f_{3\cdot(2)} \text{ is even} \end{equation*}
    (ii) (Induction Assumption):
    Assume we can find an \(n\in\N\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f_{3t} \text{ is even} \end{equation*}
    for any \(t\in\N\) such that \(1\leq t\lt n\)
    (iii) \(\mathbf{\left(\text{Prove: }P(n)\right)}\)
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} f_{3n}\amp=f_{3n-1}+f_{3n-2}\\ \amp=f_{3n-2}+f_{3n-3}+f_{3n-2}\\ \amp=2(f_{3n-2})+f_{3(n-1)} \end{align*}
    Hence, by induction assumption, \(f_{3(n-1)}\) is even. Thus by definition of even, we can find an integer \(m\) such that \(f_{3(n-1)}=2m\)
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} f_{3n}\amp=2f_{3n-2}+2m\\ \amp=2(f_{3n-2+m}) \end{align*}
    Thus by definition of even, \(f_{3n}\) is even. Thus by proof by PCI, \(f_{3n}\) is even for all \(n\in \mathbb{N}\)

    Example 6.4.5.

    Prove: For any \(m\in\N\) such that \(m\geq 1\) we have \(f_{3m+1}\) is odd

    Proof.

    (i) (Base Case):
    Since, again, we are to show this for any \(m\geq 1\text{,}\) our first base case is 1!
    \begin{equation*} f_{3(1)+1}=f_4=f_3+f_2=(f_2+f_1)+f_2=(1+1)+1=3 \end{equation*}
    Since 1 is an integer and \(3=1\cdot(1)+1\) we have that 3 is odd.
    As we are using PCI we need to show our next base case of 2.
    \begin{equation*} f_{3\cdot (2)+1}=f_7=f_6+f_5=13 \end{equation*}
    (see Example6.4.2 for the complete calculation of \(f_7\)) and since 6 is an integer and thus \(13=2\cdot 6+1\) is odd.
    Hence, we have estableshed the base cases
    \begin{equation*} P(1):f_{3\cdot(1)+1}\text{ is odd} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} P(2):f_{3\cdot(2)+1}\text{ is odd} \end{equation*}
    (ii) (Induction Assumption):
    Assume we can find an \(n\in\N\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f_{3t+1} \text{ is odd} \end{equation*}
    for any \(t\in\N\) such that \(1\leq t\lt n\)
    (iii) \(\mathbf{\left(\text{Prove: }P(n)\right)}\)
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} f_{3n+1}\amp=f_{3n}+f_{3n-1}\\ \amp=f_{3n}+f_{3n-2}+f_{3n-3}\\ \amp=2(f_{3n})+f_{3n-2}+f_{3(n-1)}\\ \amp=2(f_{3n})+f_{3(n-1)+1}+f_{3(n-1)} \end{align*}
    Note: we know \(f_{3n}\) and \(f_{3(n-1)}\) are even, because we just proved it. By our induction assumption, \(f_{3(n-1)+1}\) is odd. Hence by definition of odd, we can find \(l,m,a\in \mathbb{Z}\) such that \(f_{3n}=2l\text{,}\) \(f_{3(n-1)}=2m\text{,}\) and \(f_{3(n-1)+1}=2a+1\text{.}\)
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} f_{3n}+f_{3(n-1)+1}+f_{3(n-1)}\amp=2l+2a+1+2m\\ \amp=2(l+a+m)+1 \end{align*}
    Thus by definition of odd, \(f_{3n+1}\) is odd. Thus by proof by PCI, \(f_{3n+1}\) is odd for all \(n\in \mathbb{N}\)
