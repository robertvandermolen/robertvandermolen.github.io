    Section 3.4 More Direct Proof Examples

    We will give each of these examples a subsection, then further subdivide the sections for their in-depth explanations.

    Subsection 3.4.1 Direct Proof Example 2

    Prove: For all integers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(x\) and \(y\) are both odd then \(x+y\) is even.
    Before we begin any proof we sing our song (play-along):
    "What’s the \({\color{red}{P}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 1.
    \(x\) and \(y\) are both odd
    "What’s the \({\color{blue}{Q}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 2.
    \(x+y\) is even
    "What’re the definitions?"
    Answer 3.
    Odd (Definition 3.1.2) and even (Definition 3.1.1)!
    "Now, what to do?"
    Answer 4.
    A direct proof! (it is the only method we know so far)
    The Breakdown:
    \begin{equation*} {\color{orange}{\forall x,y\in\Z}}\text{ if }{\color{red}{x \text{ and }y\text{ are both odd}}}\text{ then } {\color{blue}{x+y\text{ is even}}} \end{equation*}

    Subsection \({\color{red}{\text{The Beginning}}}\)

    Just as before we begin by "shoveling off" our \({\color{orange}{\text{beginning quantifier}}}\) (Section 2.4) by saying something along the lines of:
    "Choose arbitrary integers \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\)"
    Now, we take care of the \({\color{red}{\text{beginning}}}\) of our direct proof...
    Assume the \({\color{red}{P}}\)
    So in our example we would write:
    "Assume both \(x\) and \(y\) are odd."

    Subsection \({\color{green}{\text{The Muddle}}}\)

    Again, as will be quite often even after this class, we do not have much but the definitions (Subsection 3.1.2), so we will apply these definitions (making note they involve quantifiers Figure 2.4.1).
    To apply the definition of odd (Definition 3.1.2) to our integer \(x\) we use the exists from the proof to produce a new integer which will need a name, lets name it \(t\text{.}\) This \(t\) can be found so that \(x=2t+1\text{.}\)
    Some questions that often arise here are: "but the proof says \(k\text{?}\)" Yet, in fear of sounding like a broken record we are using/applying the definition of odd, and when using the \(\exists\) (by Figure 2.4.1) we can find some integer (in this example) to do the job. The \(k\) in the definition is just a stand-in for some integer.
    So for our proof we could write:
    "By the definition of odd we can find an integer \(t\) so that \(x=2t+1\text{.}\)"
    Next, we do the exact same procedure with the \(y\text{,}\) but because \(y\) is a new number and it is a brand new application of the definition of odd we can produce a a new integer (Figure 2.4.1), so this time lets name it \(s\text{.}\)
    That is, in our proof we could write:
    "Again, by the definition of odd, we can find an integer \(s\) such that \(y=2s+1\text{.}\)"
    Having successfully applied our definitions we now have something we can calculate with, so lets try to add what is in our \({\color{blue}{Q}}\text{,}\) namely \({\color{blue}{x+y}}\)
    \begin{align*} {\color{blue}{x+y}}\amp=(2t+1)+(2s+1)\\ \amp=2s+2t+1+1\\ \amp=2s+2t+2\\ \amp=2(s+t+1) \end{align*}
    Now, we can (hopefully) see our definition of even at the last equality, signalling that we are ready for our \({\color{blue}{\text{end}}}\text{.}\)

    Subsection \({\color{blue}{\text{The End}}}\)

    From the \({\color{green}{\text{muddle}}}\text{,}\) we have:
    \begin{equation*} x+y=2{\color{purple}{(s+t+1)}} \end{equation*}
    To be able to make our conclusion
    \begin{equation*} {\color{blue}{Q}}:\text{ } x+y \text{ is even} \end{equation*}
    we must, again, use the definition of even (Definition 3.1.1), which uses an existential quantifier. So we must (Section 2.4) be able to produce/point at an integer, so that \(x+y\) is this integer times 2.
    We have exactly that, because (Subsection 3.1.1) \({\color{purple}{s+t+1}}\) is an integer!
    To write this in our proof we can say:
    "Since \({\color{purple}{s+t+1}}\) is an integer, by definition of even, \(x+y\) is even."
    Finally, we should end every proof with a wrap-up sentence. This sentence is to summarize the proof, most importantly laying out your proving method, in this example a direct proof.
    For this proof our wrap-up could look like:
    "Since we assumed that an arbitrary two integers \(x\) and \(y\) were odd and showed that \(x+y\) is even, we can conclude that for all integers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) whenever \(x\) and \(y\) are odd then \(x+1\) is even, by direct proof."
    Now let’s see everything written together in a single spot.
    Example 3.4.1.
    Prove: For all integers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(x\) and \(y\) are both odd then \(x+y\) is even
    Choose arbitrary integers \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) Assume both \(x\) and \(y\) are odd. By the definition of odd we can find an integer \(t\) so that \(x=2t+1\text{.}\) Again, by the definition of odd, we can find an integer \(s\) such that \(y=2s+1\text{.}\)
    Now calculate:
    \begin{align*} {\color{blue}{x+y}}\amp=(2t+1)+(2s+1)\\ \amp=2s+2t+1+1\\ \amp=2s+2t+2\\ \amp=2(s+t+1) \end{align*}
    Since \({\color{purple}{s+t+1}}\) is an integer, by definition of even, \(x+y\) is even.
    Since we assumed that an arbitrary two integers \(x\) and \(y\) were odd and showed that \(x+y\) is even, we can conclude that for all integers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) whenever \(x\) and \(y\) are odd then \(x+1\) is even, by direct proof.

    Subsection 3.4.2 Direct Proof Example 3

    The last two examples had even in the \({\color{red}{P}}\) and even in the \({\color{blue}{Q}}\text{.}\) In fear of students thinking this is some sort of immutable law/pattern lets change that pattern this time.
    Prove: For all integers \(x\) and \(y\) if \(x\) and \(y\) are both odd then \(x\cdot y\) is odd.
    Before we begin any proof we sing our song (play-along):
    "What’s the \({\color{red}{P}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 1.
    \(x\) and \(y\) are both odd
    "What’s the \({\color{blue}{Q}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 2.
    \(x\cdot y\) is odd
    "What’re the definitions?"
    Answer 3.
    Odd (Definition 3.1.2)!
    "Now, what to do?"
    Answer 4.
    A direct proof! (it is the only method we know so far)
    The Breakdown:
    \begin{equation*} {\color{orange}{\forall x,y\in\Z}}\text{ if }{\color{red}{x \text{ and }y\text{ are both odd}}} \text{ then }{\color{blue}{x\cdot y \text{ is odd.}}} \end{equation*}

    Subsection \({\color{red}{\text{The Beginning}}}\)

    We begin by taking care of our leading quantifier, \({\color{orange}{\forall x,y\in\Z}}\text{,}\) trying our best to never forget Figure 2.4.1. To do this we could use a variety of english statements to do this, for example:
    • "Select two random integers \(x\) and \(y\)"
    • "Choose two arbitrary integers \(x\) and \(y\)"
    • "Let \(x\) and \(y\) be integers"
    Next, we do the \({\color{red}{\text{first step}}}\) of a direct proof, that is, \({\color{red}{\text{assume the }P}}\text{.}\) From our song we already know this, and that is:
    "Assume \(x\) and \(y\) are both odd"

    Subsection \({\color{green}{\text{The Muddle}}}\)

    Now, as we see in this first class of proofs so often, the only tools we really have at our disposal are the definitions from Subsection 3.1.2. In this proof we have to apply a definition twice from our previous line/assumption namely:
    • \(x\) is odd
    • \(y\) is odd
    Something that bears repeating over and over and over and over again, is to always remember Figure 2.4.1. Right now we need to remember this because the definition of odd (Definition 3.1.2) invokes an existential quantifier. So to apply the definition of odd for \(x\) we can use language like:
    "We can find an \(a\in\Z\) so that \(x=2a+1\text{.}\)"
    To use the definition of odd for our \(y\) we can use language like:
    "We can also find a \(b\in\Z\) so that \(y=2b+1\text{.}\)"
    Now that we have used our definitions we can move to our usual \({\color{green}{\text{muddle}}}\) where we use some good ol’ college algebra. In a more general look at proofs as a whole, you should keep in mind what we are trying to conclude, i.e. the \({\color{blue}{Q}}\text{,}\) namely that \({\color{blue}{x\cdot y\text{ is odd}}}\text{.}\) So we should calculate \({\color{blue}{x\cdot y}}\text{.}\)
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} {\color{blue}{x\cdot y}}\amp=(2a+1)\cdot(2b+1)\\ \amp=4ab+2a+2b+1\\ \amp=2(2ab+a+b)+1 \end{align*}

    Subsection \({\color{blue}{\text{The End}}}\)

    From the \({\color{green}{\text{muddle}}}\) we now have:
    \begin{equation*} x\cdot y=2{\color{purple}{(2ab+a+b)}}+1 \end{equation*}
    Now, using the definition of odd (Definition 3.1.2) and of course Figure 2.4.1 we can conclude from the \({\color{green}{\text{muddle}}}\) that \(x\cdot y\) is odd, as \(2ab\) is an integer and thus \({\color{purple}{(2ab+a+b)}}\) is an integer, and hence we have that \(x\cdot y\) is an integer times 2 then plus one.
    Now let’s see everything written together in a single spot, with a proper wrap-up.
    Example 3.4.2.
    Prove: For all integers \(x\) and \(y\) if \(x\) and \(y\) are both odd then \(x\cdot y\) is odd.
    Select two random integers \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) Assume that \(x\) is odd and \(y\) is also odd. By definition of even we can find an integer \(a\) so that \(x=2a+1\text{.}\) Also, by the definition of odd we can find an integer \(b\) so that \(y=2b+1\text{.}\) Now we calculate:
    \begin{align*} x\cdot y\amp=(2a+1)\cdot(2b+1)\\ \amp=4ab+2a+2b+1\\ \amp=2(2ab+a+b)+1 \end{align*}
    Now since \(2ab+a+b\) is an integer, we see that \(x\cdot y\) is odd.
    To summarize, since we chose arbitrary integers \(x\) and \(y\) which we assumed were both odd, respectively, by direct proof we proved that for all integers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(x\) is odd and \(y\) is odd, then \(x\cdot y\) is odd. \(\square\)

    Subsection 3.4.3 Direct Proof Example 4

    Now let’s see an example that is not even nor odd.
    Prove: \(\forall x,y\in\Z\) if \(5|x\) and \(5|y\) then \(5|(x-2y)\)
    Before we begin any proof we sing our song (play-along):
    "What’s the \({\color{red}{P}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 1.
    \(5|x\) and \(5|y\)
    "What’s the \({\color{blue}{Q}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 2.
    \(5|(x-2y)\)
    "What’re the definitions?"
    Answer 3.
    divides (Definition 3.1.3)
    "Now, what to do?"
    Answer 4.
    A direct proof! (it is the only method we know so far)
    The Breakdown:
    \begin{equation*} {\color{orange}{\forall x,y\in\Z}}\text{ } {\color{red}{5|x \text{ and } 5|y}} \implies {\color{blue}{5|(x-2y)}} \end{equation*}

    Subsection \({\color{red}{\text{The Beginning}}}\)

    This proof is the same as we have been doing at the beginning, as it starts with our \({\color{orange}{\text{beginning quantifier}}}\text{.}\) As usual we take care of that using:
    "Let \(x\) and \(y\) be integers."
    As we are still doing a direct proof, our first step involving this is \({\color{red}{\text{Assume the }P}}\text{,}\) and for this example it is:
    "Assume that both \(5|x\) and \(5|y\text{.}\)"

    Subsection \({\color{green}{\text{The Muddle}}}\)

    In this example we go to our definitions again (being careful for the quantifier Figure 2.4.1), specifically for \(5|x\) we use the definition of divides (Definition 3.1.3) as follows:
    "By definition of divides we can find an \(a\in\Z\) so that \(x=5a\text{.}\)"
    For \(5|y\) we apply the definition of divides (Definition 3.1.3) as:
    "By definition of divides we can find a \(b\in\Z\) so that \(y=5b\text{.}\)"
    Something I have seen students do here is write "exists an integer \(b\text{.}\)" You are using the existential quantifier to produce a specific integer (Figure 2.4.1), by saying exists you are just telling me that you could theoretically find one, not telling that you are producing one to use.
    So now that we have something we can use, like usual we can calculate:
    \begin{align*} {\color{blue}{(x-2y)}}\amp=(5a)-(2\cdot(5b))\\ \amp=5(a-2b) \end{align*}

    Subsection \({\color{blue}{\text{The End}}}\)

    From the\({\color{green}{\text{ muddle }}}\)we have
    \begin{equation*} x-2y=5{\color{purple}{(a-2b)}} \end{equation*}
    and then we can reference a couple of our assumptions from Subsection 3.1.1, and say:
    "Since \(2b\) is an integer a \({\color{purple}{a-2b}}\) is an integer by the definition of divides 5 divides \(x-2y\text{.}\)"
    Now let’s see everything written together in a single spot, with an appropriate wrap-up.
    Example 3.4.3.
    Prove: \(\forall x,y\in\Z\) if \(5|x\) and \(5|y\) then \(5|(x-2y)\)
    Let \(x\) and \(y\) be integers. Assume that 5 divides both \(x\) and \(y\text{.}\) Next, we calculate:
    \begin{align*} {\color{blue}{(x-2y)}}\amp=(5a)-(2\cdot(5b))\\ \amp=5(a-2b) \end{align*}
    Since \(2b\) is an integer it follows that \(a-2b\) is an integer, thus by the definition of divides, 5 divides \(x-2y\text{.}\)
    To summarize, since we chose arbitrary integers \(x\) and \(y\) and assume that 5 divides both of them, and have shown that \(5|(x-2y)\text{,}\) by direct proof we have shown that for any integers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(5|x\) and \(5|y\) then \(5|(x-2y)\text{.}\)\(\square\)

    Subsection 3.4.4 Direct Proof Example 5

    Our last detailed example of a direct proof is a classic inequality. Inequalities will play a large role in your future analysis class.
    Prove: For all positive real numbers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(a\lt b\) then \(b^2-a^2\gt 0\text{.}\)
    Before we begin any proof we sing our song (play-along):
    "What’s the \({\color{red}{P}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 1.
    \(a\lt b\)
    "What’s the \({\color{blue}{Q}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 2.
    \(b^2-a^2\gt 0\)
    "What’re the definitions?"
    Answer 3.
    \(x\) is positive mean \(x\gt 0\)
    "Now, what to do?"
    Answer 4.
    A direct proof! (it is the only method we know so far)
    The Breakdown:
    \begin{equation*} {\color{orange}{\forall x,y\in\R \text{ with }x\gt0\text{ and }y\gt0}}\text{ }{\color{red}{a\lt b}}\implies{\color{blue}{0\lt (b^2-a^2)}} \end{equation*}

    Subsection \({\color{red}{\text{The Beginning}}}\)

    This beginning is the same as the others. We take care of the \({\color{orange}{\text{beginning quantifier}}}\) by:
    "Let \(x\) and \(y\) be positive real numbers."
    For our approach of a direct proof we need to start by \({\color{red}{\text{assuming the }P}}\text{,}\) that is:
    "Assume that \(a\lt b\)"

    Subsection \({\color{green}{\text{The Muddle}}}\)

    Now, as is in most inequality proofs first we need to work backwards. That is notice:
    \begin{equation*} b^2-a^2=(b-a)(b+a) \end{equation*}
    by our allowed assumptions of basic college algebra. But we have that \(b+a\gt 0\) since both \(a\gt 0\text{,}\) and \(b\gt 0\text{,}\) again with our assumptions of college algebra. That is we can, lets say, add \(b\) to the left side of \(a\gt 0\) and add 0 to the right side. Also, \(b-a\gt 0\) since subtracting \(a\) from both sides of our assumption results in \(a\lt b\text{.}\)

    Subsection \({\color{blue}{\text{The End}}}\)

    To write our proof in a logical format we will have to go the opposite direction, as otherwise we will have said our conclusion before we concluded it, essentially falling for circular reasoning, that is:
    Subtracting \(a\) from both sides of our assumption \(a\lt b\) we see that \(b-a\gt 0\text{,}\) and by adding \(b\) to the left and 0 to the right of \(a\gt 0\) we get that \(b+a\gt 0\text{.}\) Finally, since \(b+a\gt0\) we can multiply both sides of \(b-a\gt0\) by \((b+a)\) and arrive at:
    \begin{equation*} b^2-a^2=(b-a)(b+a)\gt0. \end{equation*}
    Now let’s see everything written together in a single spot.
    Example 3.4.4.
    Prove: For all positive real numbers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) if \(a\lt b\) then \(b^2-a^2\gt 0\text{.}\)
    Let \(x\) and \(y\) be positive real numbers. Assume that \(a\lt b\text{.}\)
    Since subtracting \(a\) from both sides of our assumption \(a\lt b\) we see that \(b-a\gt 0\text{,}\) and by adding \(b\) to the left and 0 to the right of \(a\gt 0\) we get that \(b+a\gt 0\text{.}\) Finally, since \(b+a\gt0\) we can multiply both side of \(b-a\gt0\) by \((b+a)\) and arrive at:
    \begin{equation*} b^2-a^2=(b-a)(b+a)\gt0. \end{equation*}
    Since we assumed \(x\) and \(y\) are positive real numbers and \(a\lt b\text{,}\) then we showed that \((b^2-a^2)\gt 0\text{,}\) by direct proof we can conclude, that for all positive real numbers \(x\) and \(y\) if \(a\lt b\) then \((b^2-a^2)\gt 0\text{.}\) \(\square\)
