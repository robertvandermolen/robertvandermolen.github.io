Example 6.5.5.

Prove: Every natural number \(n\gt1\) has a prime factor.

Proof.

Choose an arbitrary \(n\in\N\text{,}\) if \(n\) is prime then indeed \(n\) has a prime factor, namely itself. If \(n\) is composite then something other than \(n\) and \(1\) must divide \(n\text{.}\) Therefore the following set is non-empty:
\begin{equation*} R=\{m\in\N\text{ }|\text{ }m|n,\text{ }m\neq n, \text{ and }m\neq 1\} \end{equation*}
By WOP, \(R\) has a smallest element, denote this element as \(p\in T\text{.}\)
For the sake of contradiction assume that \(p\) is not prime, that is we assume that \(p\) is composite. By definition of composite we can find a divisor \(d\) such that \(d\neq 1\) and \(d\neq p\text{,}\) yet by definition of divides we can find a \(k\in\Z\) such that \(p=dk\) and we can find a \(t\in\N\) such that \(n=tp\text{,}\) thus \(n=tdk\text{,}\) therefore \(d\in R\) Yet by LemmaÂ 6.5.4 we have that \(d\leq p\) yet we assumed \(d\neq p\) hence \(d\lt p\) a contradiction to \(p\) being the smallest element of \(R\text{.}\) \(\square\)
