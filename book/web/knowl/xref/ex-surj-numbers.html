Example 7.6.3.

Consider the function \(f:\Z\times\Z\rightarrow\Z\) from Example 7.5.4 defined as
\begin{equation*} f(a,b)=3a+2b \end{equation*}
Prove: \(f\) is surjective

Proof.

The definition of surjective envolves an equality of sets, so we break our proof into two parts.
\(\mathbf{\text{Proof of }\text{Rng}(f)\subseteq\Z}\)
Let \(c\in\text{Rng}(f)\) by definition of range we can find an \((a,b)\in\Z\times\Z\) such that \(f(a,b)=c\text{,}\) yet by definition of \(f\) we have that \(f(a,b)=3a+2b\text{,}\) since both \(a\) and \(b\) are integers we can conclude that \(3a+2b\) is an integer and thus \(c\in\Z\) as desired.
\(\mathbf{\text{Proof of }\text{Rng}(f)\supseteq\Z}\)
Let \(z\in\Z\text{,}\) by Proposition 6.5.6 we can find two integers \(q\) and \(r\) such that \(0\leq r\lt 3\) such that \(q=3z+r\text{.}\) Since \(0\leq r\lt 3\) we have that \(r=0\) or \(r=1\) or \(r=2\text{,}\) splitting our proof into 3 cases
Case 1: Assume \(r=0\)
In this case since \(q\in\Z\) and 0 is also an integer we have that \(z=3q+2\cdot0\) hence \(f(q,0)=z\) and thus \(z\in\text{Rng}(f)\) as desired.
Case 2: Assume \(r=1\)
For this case note that \(r=1=3-2\) hence \(q+1\) and -1 are integers we have that \(z=3q+3-2=3(q+1)+(-1)\cdot 2\) hence \(f(q+1,-1)=z\) thus \(z\in\text{Rng}(f)\) as desired.
Case 3: Assume \(r=2\)
In this case since \(q\) and 1 are integers we have that \(z=3q+2\) hence \(f(q,1)=z\text{,}\) therfore \(z\in\text{Rng}(f)\) as desired.
Because we have shown no matter what \(r\) is that \(z\in\text{Rng}(f)\) we can conclude that \(z\in\text{Rng}(f)\)
Since we have shown both \(\text{Rng}(f)\subseteq\Z\) and \(\text{Rng}(f)\supseteq\Z\) we can conclude \(\text{Rng}(f)=\Z\text{,}\) therefore we have shown that \(f\) is surjective.
