Example 1.5.2.

Consider the next two predicates:
  • \(\displaystyle P(x):x\text{ is even}\)
  • \(\displaystyle Q(x):x\text{ is prime}\)
  • \(\displaystyle R(x):x^2=4\)
The statement
\begin{equation*} \exists!x\text{ }(P(x)\wedge Q(x)) \end{equation*}
is true in the universe \(\mathcal{U}=\mathbb{N}\) as the only number satisfying the statement \(P(x)\wedge Q(x)\text{,}\) that is \(x\) is even and prime, is \(2\in\mathbb{N}\) The proposition
\begin{equation*} \exists!x\text{ }R(x) \end{equation*}
on the other hand, is true for the universe \(\mathcal{U}=\mathbb{N}\text{,}\) as the only natural number to satisfy this propostion is 2, but not true for the universe \(\mathcal{U}=\mathbb{Z}\) as there are two numbers, namely +2 and -2 in the integers which make the predicate true.
