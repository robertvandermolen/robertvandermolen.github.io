    Section 5.6 Cross Product

    In this section we introduce the concept of cross product. This is yet another way of making new sets from old sets.

    Definition 5.6.1. Cross Product.

    Given two sets \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) we can define a new set which we will call the cross product of \(A\) and \(B\) (or the cartesian product) defined as:
    \begin{equation*} A\times B=\{(a,b)\text{ }|\text{ }a\in A\text{ and }b\in B\} \end{equation*}
    This is the collection of all ordered pairs. We explore this new construction in the following examples.

    Example 5.6.3.

    Lets look at another cross product of some sets. Calculate \(A\times B\) and \(B\times A\) given the sets \(A = \{2, G, E\}\) and \(B = \{\pi , e, P\}\)
    \begin{equation*} A\times B = \{(2, \pi), (2, e), (2, P), (G, \pi), (G, e), (G, P), (E, \pi ), (E, e), (E, P)\} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} B\times A = \{(\pi , 2), (\pi , G), (\pi , E), (e, 2), (e, G), (e, E), (P, 2), (P, G), (P, E)\} \end{equation*}
    Youve actually seen the cross product since very early on in your mathematical education. Generally, the ordered pairs you would consider were the cartesian plane, the good ol \(x\) and \(y\) axis.

    Example 5.6.4.

    This time if we consider a set Ive tried very hard to stay away from, namely the real numbers, \(\R\text{,}\) then the cartesian plane is the set \(\R\times \R\text{.}\)
    Throughout your youth you have surely seen many points illustrated on this cartesian plane, such as the elements \({\color{red}(1,3)}\text{,}\) \({\color{blue}(2,-2)}\text{,}\) and \({\color{green}(-2,-1)}\)
    With this basic understanding of the cross-product, we are ready to start proving with it.

    Example 5.6.5.

    Prove: For sets \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) \(C\text{,}\) and \(D\text{,}\) if \(A\subseteq C\) and \(B\subseteq D\) then \(A\times B\subseteq C\times D\)

    Proof.

    We begin by assuming that \(A\subseteq C\) and that \(B\subseteq D\text{.}\) Our objective is to prove that \(A\times B\subseteq C\times D\text{,}\) that is, we need to show a subset. We do this by choosing an arbitrary element, \(x\in A\times B\text{.}\)
    To understand this element, we of course refer to the definition (Definition5.6.1), from which we see the defining condition promises that we can find two elements, one from \(A\) and one from \(B\text{.}\) Name these elements, \(a\in A\) and \(b\in B\text{,}\) such that \(x=(a,b)\text{.}\)
    By our assumption that \(A\subseteq C\text{,}\) since we established \(a\in A\text{,}\) by definition of subset we get that \(a\in C\text{.}\) Similarly, by our assumption that \(B\subseteq D\) and since we established that \(b\in B\) again by the definition of subset \(b\in D\text{.}\)
    Therefore, by the definition of cross product \(x=(a,b)\in C\times D\) as desired. \(\square\)

    Example 5.6.6.

    Prove: Let \(A\) and \(B\) be sets, then \(A\times (B\cup C) = (A\times B)\cup (A\times C)\)

    Proof.

    [Want to show: \(\mathbf{A\times (B\cup C)\subseteq(A\times B)\cup(A\times C)}\)]
    We begin by choosing an arbitrary element \(x \in A \times (B\cup C)\text{.}\) By the definition of cross product, we can find \(m \in A\) and \(\ell \in B\cup C\) such that \(x = (m, \ell)\text{.}\) By definition of union, \(\ell \in B\) or \(\ell \in C\text{.}\)
    Hence \((m, \ell) \in A \times B\text{,}\) when \(\ell\in B\text{,}\) or \((m, \ell) \in A \times C\text{,}\) when \(\ell \in C\text{.}\) Thus, by definition of union, \(x=(m, \ell) \in (A \times B) \cup (A \times C)\) Thus by definition of subset, since \(x \in A\times (B\cup C)\) and \(x \in (A\times B)\cup (A\times C)\text{,}\) hence we have that \(A\times (B\cup C) \subset (A\times B)\cup (A\times C)\text{.}\)
    [Want to show: \(\mathbf{(A\times B)\cup(A\times C)\subseteq A\times (B\cup C)}\)]
    This time, let \(y \in (A \times B)\cup (A\times C)\text{.}\) Hence by definition of union, \(y \in (A\times B)\) or \(y \in (A\times C)\text{.}\) Hence, by definition of cross product, we can find \(s\in A\) and \(t \in B\) such that \(y=(s, t)\) or \(d\in A\) and \(r\in C\) such that \(y=(d, r)\text{.}\)
    Case 1: \(\mathbf{y=(s, t)}\)
    Since \(t\in B\text{,}\) by definition of union, \(t\in B\cup C\text{.}\) And since \(s\in A\text{,}\) \(y=(s, t) \in A\times(B\cup C)\text{,}\) by definition of cross product. Thus by definition of subset, since \(y \in (A\times B)\cup (A\times C)\) and \(y \in A\times (B\cup C)\text{,}\) therefore we can conclude that \((A\times B)\cup (A\times C) \subset A\times (B\cup C)\)
    Case 2: \(\mathbf{y = (d,r)}\)
    Since \(l\in C\text{,}\) by definition of union, \(r\in B\cup C\text{.}\) And since \(d\in A\text{,}\) by definition of cross product, \(y=(d,r) \in A\times (B\cup C)\text{.}\) Thus by definition of subset, since \(y \in (A\times B)\cup (A\times C)\) and \(y \in A\times (B\cup C)\text{,}\) thus we have \((A\times B)\cup (A\times C) \subset A\times (B\cup C)\)
    Thus, since both cases hold, by direct proof and proof by cases, \(A\times (B\cup C) = (A\times B)\cup (A\times C)\) \(\square\)
