    Section 4.3 Contradiction

    Our next method for proving is known as proof by contradiction. It is an extremely powerful example of an indirect proof. It plays off the concept of the excluded middle. In more plain language, a statement is either true or false and nothing between.
    The excluded middle is also known as the law / principle of the excluded third, in Latin, principium tertii exclusi. Another Latin designation for this law is tertium non datur or "no third [possibility] is given".

    Subsection 4.3.1 What is a contradiction?

    Proof by contradiction is the most different than the direct proof yet. The first big difference is that before now we have always considered the case where we were proving \(P\implies Q\text{,}\) yet this time we will be only considering proving any proposition \(R\text{.}\) This does not mean that we will not use it to prove propositions in the form of an implication (because we definitely will), it is just that we set up the proof not separating the implication as we have done before.

    Proof by Contradiction of \(R\).

    Assume \(\sim R\)
    \(\vdots\)
    Therefore \(T\)
    \(\vdots\)
    Therefore \(\sim T\)
    Hence \(T\wedge \sim T\) which is a contradiction.
    Thus \(R\)
    On first look it might also seem to not follow our title, but of course:
    \({\color{red}{\text{Beginning}}}\) -- Assume \(\sim R\)
    \({\color{green}{\text{Muddle}}}\) -- conclude \(T\) ... conclude \(\sim T\)
    \({\color{blue}{\text{End}}}\) -- Therefore \(T\wedge(\sim T)\) is a contradiction
    This proving method may also not seem to fit immediately into our argument section (Chapter 2). To see how it fits in first notice that before the conclusion this method is simply the direct proof of the statement
    \begin{equation*} (\sim R)\implies [T\wedge (\sim T)] \end{equation*}
    Yet, this is equivalent to \(R\text{.}\) To see that consider the following truth table.
    Finally, before we move on, when we want to use contradiction to prove a statement in the form of \(P\implies Q\) we will need to assume that \(\sim[P\implies Q]\text{.}\) So it may be helpful to review how to negate such a statement, using our techniques from Subsection 1.4.3
    \begin{align*} \sim[P\implies Q]\amp\equiv [\sim ((\sim P)\vee Q)]\text{ (Rob's Law)}\\ \amp \equiv [\sim(\sim P)\wedge (\sim Q)] \text{ (De Morgan's)}\\ \amp \equiv P\wedge (\sim Q) \text{ (double negation)} \end{align*}
    and hence we have the equivalence:
    \begin{equation*} \sim[P\implies Q]\equiv [P\wedge(\sim Q)] \end{equation*}

    Subsection 4.3.2 First Example of Proof by Contradiction

    For our first example of contradiction we will stick with our safety blanket of the integers.
    Prove: For all integers \(a\) and \(b\) if \(a- b\) is odd then \(a+ b\) is odd.
    Before we begin any proof we sing our song (play-along):
    "What’s the \({\color{red}{P}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 1.
    \(a-b\) is odd
    "What’s the \({\color{red}{Q}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 2.
    \(a+ b\) is odd
    "What’re the definitions?"
    Answer 3.
    even (Definition 3.1.1) and odd (Definition 3.1.2)
    "Now, what to do?"
    Answer 4.
    Proof by contradiction - so we can try and learn it!
    The Breakdown:
    \begin{equation*} {\color{orange}{\forall a,b\in\Z}}{\color{red}{\text{ if }a-b\text{ is odd then }a+b\text{ is odd.}}} \end{equation*}

    Subsection \({\color{red}{\text{The Beginning}}}\)

    To begin our proof we first take care of the \({\color{orange}{\text{beginning quantifier}}}\text{.}\) As usual we will do this by choosing arbitrary integers, that is, we will use language like:
    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be arbitrary integers.
    Note 4.3.2.
    Again, be careful, even though we will be negating we DO NOT negate the quantifier!
    Now, to begin our proof by contradiction we need to \({\color{red}{\text{ Assume }\sim R}}\)
    But \(R\) is not in our song! Yes that’s true, but \(R\) is just "what we want to prove". In this example we want to prove if \(a- b\) is odd then \(a+ b\) is odd. So we will need to assume \({\color{red}{\sim (\text{if }a-b\text{ is odd then }a+b\text{ is odd})}}\) or using our analysis above we will:
    Assume that \(a-b\) is odd yet \(a+b\) is not odd.
    Now this is false, and it is our job to confirm that, yet a reader with no warning might be very alarmed that you began your proof with such a blatantly false assumption. To quell our reader’s troubled stomach, and let them know what we’re doing, we can start the sentence with:
    For the sake of contradiction...
    Or for those more inclined to the classics we can simply at the beginning of our proof write:
    [RAA]
    which stands for reductio ad absurdum, that is, "reduction to absurdity."

    Subsection \({\color{green}{\text{The Muddle}}}\)

    Next, almost right away we can react to our knee jerk and play off our assumption with:
    Since we assumed that \(a+b\) is not odd, by Lemma 4.2.2 we have that \(a+b\) is even.
    Now, again, as if we were to knee jerk we could then apply our definitions of even (Definition 3.1.1) and odd (Definition 3.1.2) with language similar to:
    By the definition of odd we can obtain an integer \(\ell\) so that \(a-b=2\ell+1\text{.}\) As well, by the definition odd (Definition 3.1.2) we are able to select an integer \(s\) so that \(a+b=2s\text{.}\)
    Yet, this is where our quick reactions or just using the definitions we have sung in our song ends. In other examples we would look to the quintessential \(Q\) as our target to set up a calculation. Unfortunately in a proof by contradiction we are in search of a brand new proposition (which we have lovingly named \(T\)). Furthermore, we also need to find its negation (\(\sim T\)). This search is not always easy, and where to begin isn’t always clear.
    For this example we wandered in the woods and simply added the two things from our assumption together...
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} (a-b)+(a+b)\amp=(2\ell+1)+(2s)\\ \amp=2\ell+2s+1\\ \amp=2(\ell+s)+1 \end{align*}
    And similar to many proofs that came before this, we can conclude that since \(\ell+s\) is an integer, by the definition of odd, we have that
    \begin{equation*} T:\text{ }(a-b)+(a+b)\text{ is odd} \end{equation*}
    But, there is of course another way of calculating this sum with no consideration of these newly found integers, \(\ell\) and \(s\text{,}\) that is...
    \begin{equation*} (a-b)+(a+b)=(a+a)+(b-b)=2a \end{equation*}
    yet, since \(a\) is an integer, by the definition of even we have that \((a-b)+(a+b)\) is even, or in light of Lemma 4.2.2 we have:
    \begin{equation*} \sim T: \text{ }(a-b)+(a+b)\text{ is not odd} \end{equation*}
    Therefore we have stumbled upon our contradiction, our \(T\) and not \(T\text{.}\)

    Subsection \({\color{blue}{\text{The End}}}\)

    Now that we have found our contradiction of \(T\wedge (\sim T)\) we should make sure to point out this contradiction just in case our reader missed this, letting everyone know we have come to \({\color{blue}{\text{the end}}}\) of our proof by contradiction. We, of course, can do this by following numerous linguistic paths but we must choose one so:
    Yet, \((a-b)+(a+b)\) is an integer and cannot be both even and odd, which is a contradiction. Thus using proof by contradiction we have successfully shown that for all integers \(a\) and \(b\) if \(a- b\) is odd then \(a+ b\) is odd.
    As usual we collect it in our tldr boxes...
    Example 4.3.3.
    Prove: For all integers \(a\) and \(b\) if \(a- b\) is odd then \(a+ b\) is odd.
    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be arbitrary integers. For the sake of contradiction assume that \(a-b\) is odd yet \(a+b\) is not odd. Since we assumed that \(a+b\) is not odd, by Lemma 4.2.2 we have that \(a+b\) is even. By the definition of odd we can obtain an integer \(\ell\) so that \(a-b=2\ell+1\text{.}\) As well, by the definition odd (Definition 3.1.2) we are able to select an integer \(s\) so that \(a+b=2s\text{.}\) Calculate:
    \begin{align*} (a-b)+(a+b)\amp=(2\ell+1)+(2s)\\ \amp=2\ell+2s+1\\ \amp=2(\ell+s)+1 \end{align*}
    since \(\ell+s\) is an integer by the definition of odd, we have that \((a-b)+(a+b)\) is odd. Yet, we can also calculate as:
    \begin{equation*} (a-b)+(a+b)=(a+a)+(b-b)=2a \end{equation*}
    yet, since \(a\) is an integer by the definition of even we have that \((a-b)+(a+b)\) is even, or in light of Lemma 4.2.2 we have that \((a-b)+(a+b)\) is even.
    Yet, \((a-b)+(a+b)\) is an integer and cannot be both even and odd, which is a contradiction. Thus using proof by contradiction we have successfully shown that for all integers \(a\) and \(b\) if \(a- b\) is odd then \(a+ b\) is odd. \(\square\)

    Subsection 4.3.3 \(\sqrt{2}\) is Irrational

    This next proof is a proof that I believe every single student in mathematics should know and love. It is this proof that I learned very early on in my math career that shaped how I understood mathematics. I would meet people and tell them "I’m a math major," and they would say things like, "Ugh, I always hated math," and I would tell them "No, math is not what you think math is." Math is about truths. For example, take an extremely simple shape - the right triangle - literally just put two sticks at a sharp angle and connect the two edges with another stick. This third stick has a length that is not a fraction of either of the other sides. Pythagoras went to his grave believing that it must be, but we can show beyond a shadow of a doubt it is not.
    Before we jump into that proof, it will be helpful to have a lemma that we can call on, in all honesty to shorten our work load (what lemma’s are best for).

    Lemma 4.3.4.

    If 2 divides \(m^2\) then 2 divides \(m\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    Let \(m\) be an integer. We will prove this using contraposition - that is, we will assume 2 does not divide \(m\text{,}\) thus by Lemma 4.2.2 \(m\) is odd. Hence we can find an integer \(k\) such that \(m=2k+1\text{.}\)
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} m^2\amp=m\cdot m\\ \amp=(2k+1)(2k+1)\\ \amp=4k^2 +4k+1\\ \amp=2(2k^2 +2k)+1 \end{align*}
    Since \(2k^2 +2k\) is an integer, by definition of odd, \(m^2\) is odd. Hence by Lemma 4.2.2 \(m^2\) is not even. Thus by proof by contraposition, if 2 divides \(m^2\) then 2 divides \(m\text{.}\) \(\square\)
    Prove: \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational.
    Before we begin any proof we sing our song, although it is a little different when we’re using contradiction (play-along):
    "What’s the \({\color{red}{P}}\text{?}\)"
    Answer 1.
    \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational
    "What’s the \(Q\text{?}\)"
    Answer 2.
    There is no \(Q\) this time!
    "What’re the definitions?"
    Answer 3.
    Rational Definition 4.1.3, divides Definition 3.1.3, and greatest common divisor Definition 4.1.2
    "Now, what to do?"
    Answer 4.
    Proof by contradiction - because I said so!

    Subsection \({\color{red}{\text{The Beginning}}}\)

    For a proof by contradiction we need to begin by assuming what we need to prove is false, and to remind you, this could be jarring to a reader so let them know what you are doing, for example we could write:
    For the sake of contradiction assume that \(\sqrt{2}\) is rational. That is, it is not true that \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational

    Subsection \({\color{green}{\text{The Muddle}}}\)

    Now, we continue with our song by applying the definitions we can, per the definition of rational (Definition 4.1.3), we can find two integers \(p\) and \(q\) so that \(\sqrt{2} =\frac{p}{q}\) and
    \begin{equation*} T:\text{ GCD}(p,q)=1 \end{equation*}
    Next, we will do exactly what any good calculus student would do and that is "get-rid-of" that square root.
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} \text{square both sides: }2\amp=\frac{p^2}{q^2}\\ \text{multiply both sides by }q^2: 2q^2\amp=p^2 \end{align*}
    But, \(q^2\) is an integer, thus by the definition of even, \(p^2\) is even. Hence, by Lemma 4.3.4 we can conclude that \(p\) is even
    Thus, by the definition of even, we can find an integer \(m\) so that \(p=2m\text{.}\)
    Now, we can play our common replacement game.
    Calculate again:
    \begin{align*} 2q^2\amp=(2m)^2\\ \amp=4m^2\\ \text{Divide both sides by 2 } q^2\amp=2m^2 \end{align*}
    But \(m^2\) is an integer, so, by definition of even, \(q^2\) is even, and again by Lemma 4.3.4, \(q\) is even.
    Since \(2\mid p\) and \(2\mid q\text{,}\) 2 is a common divisor, and thus by definition of greatest common divisor (Definition 4.1.2) we have that \(\text{GCD}(p,q)\geq 2\text{,}\) yet this means that
    \begin{equation*} \sim T:\text{ GCD}(p,q)\neq 1 \end{equation*}

    Subsection \({\color{blue}{\text{The End}}}\)

    So now that we have found our contradiction \(T\wedge(\sim T)\) we are done, but just in case the reader missed this contradiction as you unwrapped it, let’s put it all in one place.
    Thus we have shown \(\text{GCD}(p,q)=1\) and \(\text{GCD}(p,q)\neq1\) which is a contradiction. Thus by proof by contradiction, we have shown that \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational.
    Now let’s see everything written together in a single spot.
    Example 4.3.5.
    Prove: \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational.
    For the sake of contradiction assume that \(\sqrt{2}\) is rational. That is, it is not true that \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational. By the definition of rational (Definition 4.1.3), we can find two integers \(p\) and \(q\) so that \(\sqrt{2} =\frac{p}{q}\) and \(\text{GCD}(p,q)=1\)
    Calculate:
    \begin{align*} \text{square both sides: }2\amp=\frac{p^2}{q^2}\\ \text{multiply both sides by }q^2: 2q^2\amp=p^2 \end{align*}
    But, \(q^2\) is an integer, thus by the definition of even, \(p^2\) is even. Hence, by Lemma 4.3.4 we can conclude that \(p\) is even
    Thus, by the definition of even we can find an integer \(m\) so that \(p=2m\text{.}\)
    Calculate again:
    \begin{align*} 2q^2\amp=(2m)^2\\ \amp=4m^2\\ \text{Divide both sides by 2 } q^2\amp=2m^2 \end{align*}
    But \(m^2\) is an integer, so, by definition of even, \(q^2\) is even, and again by Lemma 4.3.4, \(q\) is even.
    Since \(2\mid p\) and \(2\mid q\text{,}\) 2 is a common divisor, and thus by definition of greatest common divisor (Definition 4.1.2) we have that \(\text{GCD}(p,q)\geq 2\text{,}\) yet this means that \(\text{GCD}(p,q)\neq 1\)
    Thus we have shown \(\text{GCD}(p,q)=1\) and \(\text{GCD}(p,q)\neq1\) which is a contradiction. Thus by proof by contradiction, we have shown that \(\sqrt{2}\) is irrational.\(\square\)
